Suppose that $f$ and $g$ are real functions and $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ are such that $g$ is continuous at $b$ and $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = b ,$$ $$ \lim_{y \to b} g(y) = c ,$$ then we have $$ \lim_{x \to a} (g \circ f)(x) = c .$$ To see this, fix $\epsilon > 0$. Then there exists $\delta$ such that $$ |y - b| < \delta \implies |g(y) - c| < \epsilon .$$ At the same time, there exists $\delta'$ such that $$ |x - a| < \delta' \implies |f(x) - b| < \delta .$$ Thus when $|x - a| < \delta'$ we have $$ |x - a| < \delta' \implies |f(x) - b| < \delta \implies |g(f(x)) - c| < \epsilon .$$
